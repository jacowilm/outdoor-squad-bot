The Outdoor Squad — Brand Voice Guide

Why this exists

A brand voice guide is not for poetry. It's so anything written for The Outdoor Squad — by Nick,

by atrainer, by the VA, by Claude, by a freelancer — sounds like it came from the same place. Ina

market with ten direct competitors within walking distance, voice is one of the few moats that's

hard to copy. People don't remember what your gym promised. They remember how it made

them feel and whether they laughed.

This is a living document. Update as the brand evolves.

The one-line voice

The Outdoor Squad sounds like Nick wrote it: strength coach, comedian, Conan fan, casual

reference machine, genuinely on your side. Warm, honest, funny in a specifically nerdy way,

and never trying to sound like "a fitness brand."

The brand's voice is Nick's voice. The job of this document is to capture that well enough that

trainers, the VA, freelancers, and AI can write in it when Nick isn't the one typing.

Core voice attributes

1. Encouraging by default, never sycophantic

We praise effort, we celebrate wins, we genuinely believe in what people can do. We're not here to

make anyone feel small. But we don't blow smoke. If someone's been skipping sessions, we don't

pretend everything's fine — we call it out (kindly) and invite them back.

¢ Sounds like: "Massive effort this week, team. I saw a few of you do things on Wednesday
you couldn't have done in March. That's not the cardio gods smiling on you, that's
compound interest."
¢ Doesn't sound like: "You're all amazing! Crushing it! So proud!" (empty praise — sounds

like a yoga teacher who's just discovered Instagram)

2. Funny — specifically: nerdy, referential, dry, Australian

This is the most distinctive attribute and the easiest to get wrong. Detail in the humour playbook

below. The short version: we're not punchline comedians, we're the friend who quotes things at

you and assumes you'll catch the reference. When you don't catch it, that's part of the joke.

3. Scolding is allowed. Earned, never cruel.
When something needs to be said — about commitment, about skipping warm-ups, about
quitting on the third set — we say it. Direct. Australian. With love. The point is to hold members
toa standard they actually want to be held to.
¢ Sounds like: "Right. Thursday HiiT'N'Run had eight bookings and four people show up.
The four who turned up did the work for everyone. If you booked and didn't come, you owe
yourself one. See you tomorrow."
¢ Doesn't sound like: "We noticed some attendance issues this week and would love to see
you back when you're ready @" (limp, performative, off-brand)
« Also doesn't sound like: "If you can't be bothered, don't bother coming back" (cruel — we
coach, we don't fire)
4. Distinctly Australian, distinctly Inner West
Not bogan. Not larrikin caricature. Just the natural register of an educated Australian who lives
in Sydney's Inner West. Australian English (organise, recognise, programme, colour). Australian
idioms when they fit. References that locals get — Camperdown, Newtown, Redfern, the oval,
the Park Café, parking on Mallet St.
5. Real-world strength, not aesthetics
Our language is about capability, not appearance. Carrying the shopping. Lifting your kid.
Walking up the stairs without huffing. Being strong at 60. Heavy things sometimes need lifting;
we train so you can lift them.
¢ Sounds like: "You're not training to look good in a wedding photo. You're training to still be
carrying your own groceries when you're 75."
¢ Doesn't sound like: "Get summer-ready with our 8-week shred!" (off-brand, on every other
gym's website, attracts the wrong customer)
6. Community over hero
The Outdoor Squad isn't a stage for Nick or any individual trainer. It's a Squad. The voice
positions members as the protagonists, trainers as coaches who happen to share the journey, and
community as the real product.
¢ Sounds like: "Sarah deadlifted 70kg for the first time on Friday. The Camperdown 6am
crew lost their minds. This is what community is."
¢ Doesn't sound like: "Trained another champion toa new PB today (» #coachlife" (centres
the coach, not the member)

7. Honest about what training is
Training is hard. We don't pretend it's not. We don't promise overnight results, we don't sell six-
week miracles, we don't pretend Tuesday morning at 6am is "fun" in the conventional sense. We
tell the truth: it's hard, it's worth it, and you'll be glad you came.

¢ Sounds like: "Will you enjoy every minute? No. Will you regret turning up? Also no.

Different question entirely."

¢ Doesn't sound like: "Make fitness fun and easy with our incredible program!" (lying)
The humour playbook
This section is longer than the others because comedy is the brand's most distinctive lever and
the thing most likely to go wrong in execution. Most fitness brands try to be funny and end up
sounding like a Tuesday morning radio DJ. Our humour has actual shape.
The nerd reference layer
Nick is a nerd. The brand is a nerd. We quote books, films, games — naturally, the way they'd
come up in conversation. Not crowbarred. Not explained.
Source material that's fair game:

¢ Conan the Barbarian (especially — see Crom below)

¢ Tolkien — Lord of the Rings, Hobbit, easy crowd-pleasers

¢ Princess Bride — comfort references everyone seems to get

e Star Wars, Star Trek — broad cultural touchstones

¢ Monty Python — when the timing's right

« Dune, Game of Thrones, fantasy generally — strength and warrior themes fit naturally

e Video games — RPG mechanics ("levelling up," "boss fights," "respec"), Dark Souls

difficulty jokes, gym-as-dungeon framing

¢ Sci-fi generally

* Older Aussie comedy when context allows — Kath & Kim, The Castle
The references should:

¢ Land naturally, not feel performed

¢ Function asa self-selection filter (the right people get them; the wrong people don't notice)

¢ Reinforce the strength / community / effort themes when possible

¢ Never alienate — if you'd have to explain it, drop it
The references shouldn't be in every post. They're seasoning, not the main course.
Crom — patron deity of the bootcamp
Crom is a running gag, a brand mascot, and a piece of low-key brand mythology. For the
uninitiated: Crom is the god worshipped by Conan the Barbarian — astern god of strength who
has no time for the weak and demands you prove yourself in this life because he doesn't care
about the next.
This is, frankly, perfect for an outdoor strength bootcamp.
Examples:
« "Crom does not look kindly on skipped warm-ups."
e "By Crom, that was a heavy kettlebell."
« "Cool morning today. Crom approves. Crom thinks you're soft. Both true."
e "Saturday session under the trees. Crom is watching."
« New members can be welcomed to the order of Crom. PRs can be celebrated as offerings.
The cold can be blamed on Crom. Etc.
Why this works strategically (not just comedically):
1. It's specific and ownable. No other Inner West gym is doing this.
2. It reinforces positioning. Strength, grit, no excuses — without being earnest about any of
it.
3. It builds insider community. Members who get the joke feel like they're in on something.
4. It gives Nick and trainers a vocabulary to use repeatedly without it getting old.
5. It's repeatable. Crom shows up across email, social, in-person, merch potential, member
nicknames, etc.
Use regularly. Don't overdose. It should feel like an ongoing in-joke, not a marketing campaign
with a theme.
Deliberate mispronunciation
Nick mispronounces things on purpose. It's a verbal tic that translates to writing through
unusual spellings, hammy phonetic versions, or weird emphasis.
e "Yo-gah" or "Yogi" instead of yoga
¢ "Puh-lah-tees" with stretched vowels
¢ "Hye-rox" with vaguely Germanic flair

Robo-Nick — the self-aware automation
Nick's CRM automations (in Momence) are personified as Robo-Nick because no one believes a
6am follow-up email is from a real human anyway. Instead of pretending the automation is
personal, the brand admits it.
Examples:
« "G'day, this is Robo-Nick. The real Nick is either coaching, asleep, or at the coffee shop. He'll
be in touch properly soon, but in the meantime — your trial pass is ready..."
e "Robo-Nick reporting in. Real Nick is busy honouring Crom. Here's your reminder for
Wednesday's session..."
¢ Subject lines: "Robo-Nick has a question" / "From the desk of Robo-Nick" / "Robo-Nick
noticed something"
Why this works (and why it's strategically smarter than it looks):
e Inverts the standard fake-personalisation tactic that everyone's seen through anyway
¢ Builds more trust by admitting the artifice — counterintuitive but real
¢ Gives all the automation a coherent voice that doesn't try to impersonate Nick badly
¢ Positions the brand as honest, slightly subversive, and self-aware
e Another in-joke members get to be inon
Important rule: Robo-Nick handles automation and warmth-at-scale. Real Nick handles
personal, sensitive, or high-touch comms. Don't blur this. If a message would feel cold coming
from a robot, it shouldn't come from Robo-Nick.

Vocabulary do/don't
Use freely: mate, reckon, having a crack, on the tools, take the mickey, proper (as adjective: "a
proper one"), by Crom, the Squad, training (not "working out"), strong (not "shredded"), build
(not "burn"), session (not "workout"), members (not "clients"), team (not "tribe"), Robo-Nick,
real Nick
Use carefully: "fair dinkum" (sparingly — can feel costume-y), banter (overused), epic
(devalued)
Avoid: "y'all," "guys" overused, American spelling, "vibes," "energy" used spiritually,
"transformation," "shred," "summer body," "snatched," "blessed," "manifest," LinkedIn-isms
("leverage," "synergy," "unlock potential"), "amazing"/"incredible"/"absolutely" as filler, fitness
industry buzzwords ("functional patterning," "metabolic conditioning protocol"), excessive
emoji, hustle culture language

Pin descriptions are SEO-driven, so the voice gets dialled back in favour of keyword clarity.

Email — Robo-Nick vs Real Nick

Robo-Nick handles automated sequences (welcome, trial follow-up, lapsed member, birthday,
etc.). Voice is self-aware, slightly cheeky, transparent about being a robot. Real Nick sends
manual, conversational, less frequent emails. Sounds more directly Nick-like — warmer, less
formatted, occasional reference, no Robo-Nick framing.

Member comms (in-person, post-session, in-app)

Where the voice lives most naturally. Trainers should feel free to use the Crom shtick, the
references, the affectionate roasting. This is where culture is built.

Press / formal contexts

Voice dials down but doesn't disappear. Even a media quote should sound like Nick rather than a
generic gym owner. Keep the warmth and honesty; ease off the in-jokes.

Common pitfalls

1. Trying to be funny in every sentence. Humour lands because it's intermittent. Constant
jokes = exhausting. Three good lines and one warm one beats five mediocre lines.

2. Forcing the Crom reference. Every third post = brand schtick. Once a fortnight +
occasional invocations = brand lore.

3. Defaulting to fitness industry voice when in doubt. Unsure writers reach for the safe
register — "transformation," "results," "amazing." That's never us. When in doubt, write
less, write plainer, write more like Nick would say it out loud.

4. Over-explaining the joke. If a reference doesn't land for someone, that's fine. Don't gloss it.

5. Confusing "Australian" with "ocker." We're not doing Crocodile Dundee. We're doing
educated Inner West Sydney 2025.

6. Using Robo-Nick for something that should come from real Nick. Sensitive comms,
complaints, personal milestones — these need the human, not the bot.

7. Letting the voice drift in long-form content. Blog posts and longer emails are where voice
fades fastest because writers default to "essay mode." Fight it. The voice should be just as
present in 800 words as in 80.

The Nick filter

Final gut check for any piece of writing: read it aloud. Does it sound like something Nick would
actually say in a session, in a coffee shop, or at the start of class? If yes, ship it. If it sounds like
something a marketing agency would say, rewrite it.
